Vietnam Draft Lottery as an IV

  • Concerns about the fairness of the conscription policy lead to the introduction of a draft lottery in 1970.

    • From 1970 to 1972 random sequence numbers were assigned to each birth date in cohorts of 19-year-olds.

    • Men with lottery numbers below a cutoff were drafted while men with numbers above the cutoff could not be drafted.

  • The draft did not perfectly determinate military service:

    • Many eligible men were exempted for health, schooling and other reasons

    • Non-eligible men volunteered for service

Note that using lotteries does NOT guarantee that we have a Randomized Control Trial (more on this later)

Generalized Potential Outcomes Framework

  • \(Y(1)\) and \(Y(0)\) are short for \(Y(T=1)\) and \(Y(T=0)\): \(T \rightarrow Y\)

  • We also have \(Y(Z=1)\) and \(Y(Z=0)\): \(Z \rightarrow Y\)

  • Now we have \(T(Z=1)\) and \(T(Z=0)\), or \(T(1)\) and \(T(0)\) for short:\(Z \rightarrow T\)

Four groups in data

The LATE Theorem

  • In general causal effects are heterogenous

  • Under the three standar IV Assumptions plus Monotonicity (No Defiers) \[ \small T(Z_i=1) \geq T(Z_i=0), \forall i \]

  • The IV estimand identifies the LATE (Local Average Treatment Effect)

  • The LATE identified by IV methods is the average causal effect of \(D\) on \(Y\) on the sub-group affected by the instrument: those who were treated due to \(Z\) but would not be treated otherwise

    • This is a generally unidentifiable subpopulation.

How Useful is LATE?

  • In most applications we would be mostly interested in estimating the average treatment effect on the whole population (ATE)

  • It is usually not possible with IV to identify ATE, without further assumptions (e.g.¬†constant causal effects): problems of external validity of the IV result.

  • LATE is not informative about effects on never-takers or always-takers because the instrument does not affect their treatment status. ## Final Comments on IV

  • The intuition of LATE generalizes to cases with continuous endogenous variables and instruments, and additional control variables.

  • The LATE is the average effect of (endogenous treatment) \(T\) on \(Y\) for those whose treatment status has been changed by the instrument \(Z\)

Weak Instruments

  • IV can be severely biased in particular if instruments are weak and if you use many instruments

    • In the worst case, the 2SLS sampling distribution is centered on the probability limit of OLS.

  • It is important to report the first stage and test the (joint) relevance of instruments

Dif-in-Dif Example

  • In February 1992 NJ increased the state minimum wage from \(4.25\) to \(5.05\) US$, while in Pennsylvania minimum wage stayed at \(4.25\)

  • Is the change from Feb.¬†to Nov.¬†in NJ a causal effect? \[ \small E [Y_{ist} | s = NJ, t = N ] - E [Y_{ist} | s = NJ, t = F ] \]

    • NO: other factors affecting employment may have changed

  • Is the difference between NJ and PA in November a causal effect? \[ \small E [Y_{ist} | s = NJ, t = N ] - E [Y_{ist} | s = PA, t = N ] \]

    • NO: systematic differences in employment may exist between them
    • NJ may be “self-selected” into the treatment of changing minimum wage

Differences-in-Differences

  • The differences-in-differences strategy amounts to comparing the change in employment in NJ to the change in employment in PA. \[ \small \begin{align*} &\left( E [Y_{ist} | s = NJ, t = N ] - E [Y_{ist} | s = NJ, t = F ] \right) \\ -&\left( E [Y_{ist} | s = PA, t = N ] - E [Y_{ist} | s = PA, t = F ] \right) \end{align*} \]

  • Or equivalently (re-arranging terms), comparing the difference in employment between NJ and PA after with the same difference before

\[ \small \begin{align*} &\left( E [Y_{ist} | s = NJ, t = N ] - E [Y_{ist} | s = PA, t = N ] \right) \\ -&\left( E [Y_{ist} | s = NJ, t = F ] - E [Y_{ist} | s = PA, t = F ] \right) \end{align*} \]

Regression Diff-in-Diff

  • Advantages:

    • It is easy to calculate standard errors.
    • We can control for other variables which may reduce the residual variance (lead to smaller standard errors).
    • It is easy to include multiple periods.
    • We can study treatments with different treatment intensity. (e.g.¬†varying increases in the minimum wage for different states).

  • The typical regression model that we estimate is: \[ \small Outcome_{it} = \beta_1 + \beta_2 Treat_i + \beta_3 Post_t + \color{red}{\beta_4} (Treat * Post)_{it} + \varepsilon_{it} \]

    • \(Treat\) = a dummy if the observation is in the treatment group
    • \(Post\) = post-treatment dummy (“after”)

Key Assumption: Common Trends

  • The key assumption for any Diff-in-Diff strategy is that the outcome in treatment and control group would follow the same time trend in the absence of the treatment.

  • They just follow parallel paths

  • Common trend assumption is difficult to verify but one often uses pre-treatment data to show that the trends are the same.

    • Using leads and lags in regression models

  • Even if pre-trends are the same one still has to worry about other policies changing at the same time.

Key Assumption: Common Trends (cont‚Äôd)

  • This method is intended to give a proper counterfactual

  1. \((NJ Post - \color{red}{NJ Pre) - (PA Post - PA Pre) }\)

    • the second difference “purge” the common trend

  2. \((NJ Post - \color{red}{PA Post) - (NJ Pre - PA Pre) }\)

    • the second difference “purge” the original systematic differences (that remains given the common evolution in the absence of treatment)
  • This assumption is often more credible, conditional on additional regressors

\[ \small Y_{ist} = \alpha+ \gamma T_s + \lambda d_t + \delta (T_s d_t) + \beta X_{ist} + \varepsilon_{ist} \]

  • When controlling for other characteristics, we assume common trends ceteris paribus

Standard Errors in Diff-in-Diff Strategies

  • As pointed out before, many papers using a Diff-in-Diff strategy use data from many years (not only one pre and one post period).

  • The variables of interest in many of these setups only vary at a group level (say, state)

  • Bertrand, Duflo, and Mullainathan (2004) propose the following solutions:

    1. Clustering standard errors at the group level.
    2. Aggregating the data into one pre and one post period.

  • Correct treatment of standard errors sometimes makes the number of groups very small

Synthetic Control Methods

  • Abadie & Gardeazabal (2003) pioneered a synthetic control method when estimating the effects of the terrorist conflict in the Basque Country using other Spanish regions as a comparison group.

  • Card (1990) implicitly used a very similar approach in his Mariel boatlift paper investigating the effect of immigration on employment of natives.

  • The basic idea behind synthetic controls is that a combination of units often provides a better comparison for the unit exposed to the intervention than any single unit alone.

    • It is important to “show” that the combined control group is ex-ante similar to the treatment group.

The Basque Country is Different

The Synthetic Control Method

  • They have \(J\) available control regions (the 16 Spanish regions other than the Basque Country).

  • They want to assign weights \(W = (w_1, ..., w_J )'\) to each region with \(w_j \ge0\) and \(\sum w_j = 1\)

  • This ensures that there is no extrapolation outside the support of the growth predictors for the control regions.

  • The weights are chosen so that the synthetic Basque country most closely resembles the actual one before terrorism by minimizing: \[ \small (X_1- X_0 W)'V(X_1- X_0 W) \]

  • Implemented in Stata and R: library synth
